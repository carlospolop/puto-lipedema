## Additional evidence from Task 1

### LIPODIET primary clinical RCT
- Lundanes et al. 2024, *Obesity*.
- PMID 38627016; DOI 10.1002/oby.24026; trial NCT04632810.
- Randomized open-label parallel RCT, n=70 females with lipedema and obesity.
- 8 weeks, both 1200 kcal/day and 60 g/day protein.
- Low-carb: 75 g/day carbohydrate, 25 E% CHO, 55 E% fat.
- Low-fat: 180 g/day carbohydrate, 60 E% CHO, 20 E% fat.
- LCD caused greater weight loss (between-group −2.8 kg; p<0.001) and greater reduction in “pain now” (−1.1 points vs low-fat; p=0.009). QoL improved in both groups. Pain reduction was not explained by weight loss alone.
- Limitations: short term, open label, obesity population, low-carb lost more weight despite energy prescription.

### Additional LIPODIET analyses
- PMID 39574523 / PMCID PMC11578713 / DOI 10.3389/fnut.2024.1484612: MRI sub-study n=13; LCD subgroup n=5. LCD reduced calf SAT area, calf circumference and pain; both groups lost weight, fat mass, fat-free mass and calf muscle area. Muscle preservation matters.
- PMID 40125475 / PMCID PMC11929874 / DOI 10.1016/j.cdnut.2025.104571: cytokines/fibrotic factors. LCD-only reductions in MIP-1β, TNF-α, hsCRP, but no significant between-group differences and no association between pain change and cytokine/fibrotic marker changes.
- PMID 39566600 / DOI 10.1016/j.clnesp.2024.11.018: appetite hormones; LCD improved fullness/postprandial ghrelin signal in secondary analysis.
- PMID 41479665 / PMCID PMC12753418 / DOI 10.3389/fnut.2025.1716592: hedonic hunger/eating behavior; LCD reduced some food-cue/hedonic hunger measures.

### Other evidence added
- PMID 36035515 / PMCID PMC9415463 / DOI 10.2147/DMSO.S377720: 16-week LCHF vs moderate-carb/moderate-fat in 91 women; larger reductions in weight/fat/circumferences in LCHF; less rigorous than RCT.
- PMID 41010539 / PMCID PMC12472235 / DOI 10.3390/nu17183014: 7-month Mediterranean-style ketogenic/LCHF intervention; CRP/IL-6 and Dietary Inflammatory Index improved; likely related Polish cohort.
- PMID 41767202 / PMCID PMC12946969 / DOI 10.1210/jcemcr/luag018: 2026 normal-weight case report; diet-induced weight loss reduced lower-body/affected fat similarly to upper-body fat; single case only.
- PMID 41310256 / DOI 10.1038/s41366-025-01960-w: cross-sectional 2026; higher Dietary Inflammatory Index/BMI associated with higher TNF-α/IL-6; higher Mediterranean Diet Score with lower inflammation; no clear pain/QoL association.

## Additional safety points from Task 2

### Cautions / do not start strict keto without medical supervision
- Pregnancy and lactation.
- Current or important history of eating disorder.
- Type 1 diabetes or insulin-dependent diabetes.
- SGLT2 inhibitors: empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin, ertugliflozin; risk of euglycemic ketoacidosis.
- Insulin or sulfonylureas: hypoglycemia risk; medication adjustment needed.
- Chronic kidney disease/eGFR reduction/albuminuria, advanced liver disease, pancreatitis, gout/hyperuricemia, gallbladder disease, familial hypercholesterolemia/high baseline LDL-C/ApoB, sarcopenia/fragility.

### Labs and follow-up
- Baseline: lipids, non-HDL-C, ApoB if possible/risk; glucose/HbA1c; optional insulin/HOMA-IR; CRP/hs-CRP; ALT/AST/GGT; creatinine/eGFR; electrolytes; uric acid if keto/gout/lithiasis.
- 2–4 weeks: mostly clinical review—blood pressure, dizziness/hypoglycemia, constipation/diarrhea/reflux, cramps/fatigue, anxiety/rigidity/binges, medication review.
- 6–12 weeks: repeat lipids/ApoB if needed, glucose/HbA1c, liver/kidney, electrolytes, uric acid when relevant.

### Muscle/fiber/electrolytes
- Protein: practical target 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day of reference or adjusted weight, individualized in kidney disease.
- Resistance training 2–4x/week when possible.
- Fiber: aim toward 25–30 g/day if tolerated, using low-carb vegetables, chia/flax/psyllium, nuts/seeds, avocado; legumes/berries/oats/pseudocereals if flexible low-carb.
- Electrolytes: keto/low-carb increases early natriuresis/diuresis; review sodium, potassium food sources, magnesium, hydration, and avoid unsupervised potassium supplements in kidney disease or RAAS blockers.

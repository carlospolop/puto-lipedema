# Source-verification report: diuretics, withdrawal edema, and pseudo-Bartter physiology

## Executive conclusions

1. **[Direct lipedema] German S2k 2024:** Recommendation 6.1 says exactly:  
   > “Diuretika sollen nicht zur Behandlung des Lipödems eingesetzt werden. Der Einsatz von Diuretika bei Lipödempatientinnen aus internistischer Indikation ist möglich.”

   This is a **strong recommendation** (`↑↑`, “sollen”) with **94.4% agreement**, classified as **Konsens**, not *Starker Konsens*. The methodological report defines *Konsens* as >75–95% agreement and *Starker Konsens* as >95%. Thus, “diuretics must not be used to treat lipedema, but may be used for an internal-medicine indication” is accurate; “diuretics are absolutely contraindicated in anyone with lipedema” is not.

2. **[Direct lipedema] English/German short versions:** The open German short article reproduces the same recommendation, grade, and 94.4% agreement. The English short article is bibliographically verified as DOI `10.1111/ddg.15513`, PMID `39188170`, but its accessible PubMed abstract does not include the diuretic recommendation and Wiley blocked the full PDF. Therefore, an exact English quotation was **not independently verified**. The authoritative exact wording remains the opened AWMF German full PDF.

3. **[Direct lipedema] US Standard of Care 2021:** Statement 2.8 reads exactly:  
   > “Long-term use of diuretics should be avoided in people with lipedema.” `(⊕B)`

   This came from a Delphi consensus process. Under the paper’s own notation, one plus sign means a strong recommendation and `B` moderate-quality evidence. However, its cited reference 78 is a PulmCCM explanatory article on the revised Starling principle—not a diuretic trial—and references 2–3 are lipedema tissue/inflammation studies, not withdrawal or treatment trials. It is therefore publication-safe to describe this as a **consensus recommendation with no direct lipedema diuretic trial behind it**, not as trial-proven efficacy or harm.

4. **[Adjacent lymphedema] ISL 2023 consensus:** It says diuretics have limited use in the initial phase of complete decongestive therapy and should be reserved for specific comorbidities or complications; long-term use is discouraged because it has marginal benefit for peripheral lymphedema and may cause fluid/electrolyte imbalance. Explicit exceptions include cavity effusions such as ascites or hydrothorax, protein-losing enteropathy, palliative care, and a short course in malignant lymphatic obstruction.

5. **[Adjacent evidence] Withdrawal edema:** Historical studies support the possibility of temporary rebound sodium/water retention or edema after stopping chronic diuretics, but none studied a lipedema cohort. Results differ substantially by population, prior indication, comorbidity, and study design. They do **not** support a universal time course, a universal percentage, or a lipedema-specific taper.

6. **[Adjacent evidence] Historical contradiction:** MacGregor’s 1975 paper involved two patients and proposed diuretic-perpetuated edema; the 1979 paper involved ten women and reported withdrawal-related sodium/water retention. Dunnigan 1987 and Pelosi et al. 1995 showed that idiopathic edema cannot be attributed to diuretic use alone. These are legitimately conflicting adjacent data, not interchangeable confirmation of a single causal theory.

7. **[Mechanism] Pseudo-Bartter:** This is a Bartter-like biochemical/clinical phenotype—typically normotensive hypokalemic, hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis with volume contraction and secondary renin/aldosterone activation—without the primary inherited Bartter tubulopathy. It is a **syndrome/pattern, not proof of medication abuse**. Causes include prescribed or nonprescribed loop/thiazide exposure, vomiting or gastric drainage, chloride-losing diarrhea or ostomy losses, cystic-fibrosis sweat salt loss, low chloride intake, and other acquired renal or extrarenal salt-loss states.

8. **[Mechanism] Urine chloride is timing-sensitive:** A spot urine chloride below or above 20 mmol/L can help classify metabolic alkalosis, but diuretic exposure may produce high or low urine chloride depending on the drug’s duration of action and sampling time. A single result neither proves nor excludes diuretic exposure; repeated clinical, biochemical, and—when indicated—drug testing is more defensible.

9. **[Mechanism/official labeling] Diuretic classes are not interchangeable:** Loop and thiazide diuretics can produce chloride-depletion/hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis. Acetazolamide causes bicarbonaturia and can cause metabolic acidosis, typically a hyperchloremic/normal-anion-gap pattern. Potassium-sparing agents such as spironolactone and amiloride carry hyperkalemia and renal-function risks. “All diuretics cause alkalosis” is false.

## Pseudo-Bartter definition and terminology

### Publication-safe definition

**[Mechanism]** Pseudo-Bartter syndrome is an acquired Bartter-like state, usually characterized by hypokalemic, hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis, volume contraction, normal or low blood pressure, and secondary activation of renin and aldosterone, without the primary inherited renal tubular defect that defines Bartter syndrome.

### Causes that do not imply abuse

- **Acquired renal chloride loss:** prescribed or nonprescribed loop diuretics; thiazide exposure may produce a more Gitelman-like pattern.
- **Extrarenal gastric loss:** vomiting or nasogastric drainage.
- **Intestinal loss:** congenital or acquired chloride-losing diarrhea, high-output ostomy, and some patterns of laxative overuse.
- **Sweat loss:** cystic fibrosis, particularly with heat exposure or infancy.
- **Reduced chloride availability:** inadequate chloride intake, sometimes combined with other susceptibility.
- **Other acquired renal disorders:** structural or inflammatory renal disease and less common salt-wasting states.

### Terminology caution

- **Bartter syndrome** should be reserved for the inherited renal salt-wasting tubulopathy unless a source explicitly uses an older phenotypic classification.
- **Pseudo-Bartter** is used inconsistently: some authors emphasize extrarenal chloride loss, while others include diuretic-induced acquired renal salt loss.
- **Pseudo-Gitelman** is sometimes used for an acquired Gitelman-like phenotype with hypomagnesemia and hypocalciuria, including thiazide-like effects, but its boundaries are not standardized.
- Consequently, the clearest publication terminology is to state the actual mechanism—“acquired renal chloride wasting during diuretic exposure” or “extrarenal chloride depletion from vomiting/sweat/intestinal loss”—rather than relying on the pseudo-Bartter label alone.

### Adjacent-evidence claims

4. **[Evidencia adyacente]**  
   “Estudios antiguos en edema idiopático y en personas mayores muestran que la retirada de diuréticos puede acompañarse de retención transitoria de sodio y agua o de un aumento temporal del edema; estos datos no proceden de cohortes de lipedema.”

5. **[Evidencia adyacente]**  
   “La magnitud y duración del edema tras la retirada varían según la indicación original, las comorbilidades, la duración del tratamiento y el diseño del estudio; no existe una cronología universal aplicable al lipedema.”

6. **[Evidencia adyacente]**  
   “Los porcentajes del 6% y 11% comunicados en una revisión reciente corresponden a dos estudios de insuficiencia cardíaca, con evidencia de baja certeza, y no deben presentarse como tasas de edema de retirada en lipedema.”

7. **[Linfedema adyacente]**  
   “El consenso de la International Society of Lymphology desaconseja la administración prolongada de diuréticos para el linfedema periférico por su beneficio marginal y el riesgo de alteraciones hidroelectrolíticas, aunque contempla usos concretos ante determinadas comorbilidades o complicaciones.”

### Mechanism claims

8. **[Mecanismo]**  
   “El pseudo-Bartter es un patrón clínico y bioquímico similar al síndrome de Bartter hereditario, pero sin la tubulopatía genética primaria. Su presencia no demuestra por sí sola abuso de diuréticos.”

9. **[Mecanismo]**  
   “El pseudo-Bartter puede aparecer por pérdidas renales o extrarrenales de cloro, entre ellas diuréticos prescritos o no prescritos, vómitos, pérdidas gastrointestinales, pérdidas salinas por sudor en fibrosis quística y otros estados de depleción de cloro.”

10. **[Mecanismo]**  
    “El cloro urinario depende del momento de la muestra: durante o después del efecto de un diurético puede ser alto o bajo. Una medición aislada no confirma ni excluye exposición a diuréticos.”

11. **[Mecanismo/etiquetado oficial]**  
    “Los diuréticos de asa y las tiazidas pueden favorecer alcalosis metabólica por depleción de cloro; la acetazolamida produce bicarbonaturia y puede causar acidosis metabólica hiperclorémica; los ahorradores de potasio presentan riesgo de hiperpotasemia, especialmente cuando existe deterioro renal.”

## Claims to avoid

- **[Unsupported]** “Los diuréticos están absolutamente contraindicados en todas las personas con lipedema.”
- **[Unsupported]** “El lipedema impide tratar con diuréticos una insuficiencia cardíaca u otra indicación médica.”
- **[Unsupported]** “Los diuréticos han demostrado en ensayos clínicos que empeoran el lipedema.”
- **[Unsupported]** “Todo el edema del lipedema se debe a inflamación.”  
  This wording appears in the US consensus but is broader than the directly cited evidence and ignores overlapping venous, lymphatic, cardiac, renal, hepatic, medication-related, or idiopathic edema.
- **[Unsupported]** “La retirada produce siempre edema durante exactamente tres semanas.”
- **[Unsupported]** “El aumento esperado es de 3,9 kg” or “3.5% of patients.”  
  The 3.9 kg value was a mean in a selected 10-patient successful-withdrawal subgroup; 3.5% was an edema-index change, not patient incidence.
- **[Unsupported]** “El riesgo de edema de retirada es del 6–11%.”  
  Those are two individual heart-failure-study results, not a general risk range.
- **[Unsupported]** “El pseudo-Bartter demuestra abuso, engaño o un trastorno alimentario.”
- **[Unsupported]** “Un cloro urinario bajo descarta diuréticos” or “un cloro alto demuestra diuréticos.”
- **[Unsupported]** “Todos los diuréticos causan alcalosis.”
- **[Unsupported]** Any exact withdrawal or taper schedule derived from these studies. None supplies a validated, lipedema-specific dosing or tapering protocol.

## Remaining uncertainties

1. No direct clinical trial was identified comparing diuretic continuation, withdrawal, or tapering specifically in people with lipedema.
2. The German guideline’s recommendation strength and agreement are verified, but its mechanistic discussion relies largely on adjacent idiopathic-edema literature and expert consensus.
3. The exact full-text English wording in DOI `10.1111/ddg.15513` remains unverified because Wiley blocked public PDF retrieval. Its PubMed abstract neither confirms nor contradicts the German recommendation because it omits individual recommendations.
4. Historical “idiopathic edema” cohorts probably included heterogeneous disorders by modern diagnostic standards. Their results should not be transferred quantitatively to lipedema.
5. Urine drug screens have assay- and timing-dependent sensitivity. A negative result cannot retrospectively establish absence of exposure over long periods.
6. “Pseudo-Bartter” and “pseudo-Gitelman” are not used uniformly across nephrology, pediatrics, eating-disorder, and cystic-fibrosis literature.
7. The ISL document is a 2023 consensus by title and journal imprint, while DOI/Crossref online registration occurred in 2024; both dates may appear in databases.

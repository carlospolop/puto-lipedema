## Superficial leg vein treatment in lipedema context

### What the literature says overall

For visible spider veins and reticular veins on the legs, sclerotherapy remains the best-supported first-line treatment in most guidelines, especially for symptomatic or cosmetically distressing CEAP C1 disease. The 2022 European Society for Vascular Surgery guideline calls sclerotherapy the “gold standard” for reticular veins and telangiectasias and recommends it as first choice for reticular veins; the 2023 Society for Vascular Surgery, American Venous Forum, and American Vein and Lymphatic Society guideline strongly recommends liquid or foam sclerotherapy for symptomatic telangiectasias and reticular veins. Transcutaneous laser is usually framed as a good alternative when the veins are very small, especially <1 mm, or when the patient has needle phobia, sclerosant allergy, telangiectatic matting, or prior sclerotherapy failure.

The evidence is not simple, however. A 2021 Cochrane review of 35 studies and 3,632 participants found that no single treatment was clearly superior overall across all outcomes, largely because studies were small, heterogeneous, and often short-term. Compared with sclerotherapy, laser showed no clear difference in overall clearance/improvement, but laser may cause less hyperpigmentation; foam did not clearly outperform liquid for C1 disease and may cause more matting. A 2023 network meta-analysis of 19 studies, 1,356 patients, and 2,051 procedures suggested that laser may be superior to injection sclerotherapy specifically for telangiectasias, while stronger sclerosants seem to trade efficacy for more adverse effects.

For people with lipedema, no lipedema-specific randomized trials comparing spider-vein sclerotherapy versus transcutaneous laser were identified. The direct lipedema literature is mostly diagnostic or observational, not interventional. Practical advice for lipedema patients must therefore be extrapolated from general chronic venous disease and dermatologic laser literature. This extrapolation should be cautious because lipedema pain, tenderness, edema from other causes, and symptom burden may persist even if cosmetically visible veins are treated successfully.

### Definitions and clinical distinction

In venous terminology and CEAP classification, telangiectasias are small intradermal veins, usually <1 mm in diameter; reticular veins are deeper, bluish subdermal veins typically <3 mm; varicose veins are ≥3 mm, dilated and tortuous. CEAP classifies telangiectasias and reticular veins as C1 and varicose veins as C2. “Spider veins” is the patient-facing synonym usually used for telangiectasias.

A feeder vein is not a formal CEAP or VEIN-TERM category. Clinically, it is a colloquial term for a slightly larger reticular vein or small tributary that supplies a network of visible spider veins. Treating only the tiny surface spider veins while ignoring the feeding reticular vein often gives incomplete results or earlier recurrence. Duplex and anatomic studies show that visible telangiectasias are frequently connected to reticular or other incompetent veins, and major guidelines recommend treating significant associated incompetent veins first.

Telangiectasias are fine red, purple, or blue surface vessels. Reticular veins are usually flatter, bluish-green, deeper, and more linear or branching. Varicose veins are larger and may bulge or become palpable, especially on standing. If a patient has visible bulging veins, edema, skin changes, or symptoms suggesting larger truncal reflux, that is no longer just a “spider vein” problem.

These distinctions matter because they map to treatment choice. Reticular veins and feeder veins are more amenable to sclerotherapy, whereas very fine superficial telangiectasias can sometimes be better suited to short-wavelength laser systems such as KTP 532 nm or PDL 585–595 nm. Nd:YAG 1064 nm penetrates deeper and is more relevant for somewhat larger or deeper vessels, including some reticular veins, although it is often more painful. Bulging varicose tributaries are generally not a surface-laser problem; guidelines favor miniphlebectomy or ultrasound-guided sclerotherapy instead.

### Guidelines and comparative studies

The SVS/AVF/AVLS 2023 guideline gives a strong recommendation, moderate-quality evidence for liquid or foam sclerotherapy in symptomatic telangiectasias and reticular veins, and a weak recommendation, moderate-quality evidence for transcutaneous laser in selected situations such as sclerosant allergy, needle phobia, treatment failure, or very small veins with matting.

The ESVS 2022 guideline places sclerotherapy first. It recommends duplex ultrasound before treating reticular veins and telangiectasias, recommends treating significant associated incompetent veins first, recommends sclerotherapy as first choice for reticular veins, and says transcutaneous laser should be considered for telangiectasias. It notes that shorter wavelengths such as KTP and PDL are usually more effective for telangiectasias, while Nd:YAG is more useful for reticular veins because of deeper penetration. It also states that Nd:YAG appears similarly effective to sclerotherapy in comparative studies, but with more pain.

The European sclerotherapy guideline recommends liquid sclerotherapy as the method of choice for C1 disease and treats foam as an alternative. It suggests typical liquid polidocanol concentrations of 0.25%–0.5% for telangiectasias and 0.5%–1% for reticular veins, though concentration, sclerosant choice, and technique vary by clinician and country. Repeated sessions improve results.

Primary comparative studies:
- Lupton et al. 2002: randomized within-patient trial, 20 patients; one leg sodium tetradecyl sulfate, other long-pulsed 1064 nm Nd:YAG. Sclerotherapy produced better/faster responses in fewer sessions. PMID 12174060; DOI 10.1046/j.1524-4725.2002.02029.x.
- Levy et al. 2004: sequential comparative study, 14 women, one session long-pulsed Nd:YAG and one session sclerotherapy in different sequence combinations. Similar overall results; Nd:YAG works but does not clearly outperform injection-based treatment. PMID 15022257; DOI 10.1002/lsm.20010.
- Munia et al. 2012: prospective within-patient study, 30 women; one leg Nd:YAG laser and other 75% glucose sclerotherapy. Similar patient-rated clearance after three sessions; sclerotherapy had advantages in cost, pain, and faster improvement. PMID 22221551; DOI 10.1111/j.1524-4725.2011.02226.x.
- Parlar et al. 2015: prospective randomized open-label trial comparing foam sclerotherapy versus Nd:YAG; both successful, but Nd:YAG more painful and especially suitable for needle phobia, sclerosant allergy, and very small veins/matting; sclerotherapy could also treat feeder veins. PMID 25069999; DOI 10.1111/jdv.12627.
- Ianosi et al. 2019: prospective randomized study, 285 women and 190 legs per method after bilateral allocation among laser, polidocanol and hypertonic saline; supports that Nd:YAG can be similarly effective but does not overturn sclerotherapy as default first-line care. PMID 30679981; DOI 10.3892/etm.2018.6985.
- KTP 532 nm: useful for very small superficial veins, especially <0.7–1.0 mm, but poor for larger/deeper vessels. PMID 20666669; PMID 24127163.
- PDL 585–595 nm: more relevant for smaller superficial red veins, not deeper blue reticular veins. PMIDs 9041501 and 10960824.
- IPL: much weaker rigorous evidence for lower-limb telangiectasias/reticular veins; Cochrane 2021 found no eligible IPL studies among included comparative evidence.

### Combined approaches

CLaCS and related strategies combine transdermal Nd:YAG with injection sclerotherapy. The rationale is to weaken/preheat the vessel with laser and finish closure chemically, especially in mixed networks of spider, reticular and feeder veins. Cochrane found low-certainty evidence that laser plus polidocanol may improve resolution compared with polidocanol alone, but with more pain and limited certainty.

Moreno-Moraga et al. 2014 compared polidocanol alone with polidocanol microfoam plus Nd:YAG, reporting much higher long-term clearance in the combination arm. Indexed summaries describe 79 legs in polidocanol-only group and 517 legs in combination group with 3-year follow-up. The asymmetry and technique/operator dependence make it promising but not definitive. PMID 23989971; DOI 10.1177/0268355513502786.

Nasser et al. 2024 reported superior efficacy and safety for CLaCS versus conventional polidocanol sclerotherapy in telangiectasias and reticular veins. PMID 38522666. This remains early evidence from specialized groups.

### Realistic outcomes, recurrence and duplex ultrasound

Patients should expect improvement, not perfection. Multiple sessions are common. Many studies used two or three sessions spaced by weeks to a couple of months.

Short-term clearance can be high in selected series, but this does not mean permanent disappearance. Recurrence is common, especially for telangiectasias. A 2026 long-term follow-up study reported telangiectasia recurrence common at 3 years regardless of treatment, while reticular veins showed much less recurrence; pigmentation largely resolved spontaneously, and matting persisted more in telangiectasias than reticular veins. PMID 41873145; DOI 10.1177/1358863X251412739.

Symptom improvement may occur in selected patients, because C1 disease can produce pain, burning, itching or bleeding. But if symptoms are heavy painful legs, diffuse tenderness, edema or generalized skin sensitivity, visible spider veins may only be one component. Treating them may improve focal burning/bleeding or appearance, but may do little for whole-leg pain driven by lipedema, truncal reflux, lymphatic issues or overlap.

On duplex ultrasound, ESVS 2022 recommends DUS before treating reticular veins/telangiectasias because significant associated incompetent veins should be identified and treated first. SVS/AVF/AVLS 2023 says DUS should not be routine for asymptomatic C1 disease because it may trigger unnecessary truncal ablation, but may be performed in symptomatic C1 patients with bleeding or severe pain/burning to exclude associated incompetence. Clinical synthesis: if feeder/refluxing veins could change management, imaging helps; if purely cosmetic, routine extensive scanning is less justified.

For lipedema-like patients, DUS becomes more reasonable with edema, heaviness, aching beyond visible spider veins, prior vein treatment, recurrent veins, medial thigh clusters suggesting GSV association, bulging veins, skin changes or suspected chronic venous insufficiency.

Aftercare: older randomized studies support compression after C1 sclerotherapy. Kern et al. 2007 found 3 weeks of 23–32 mmHg compression improved appearance after telangiectasia/reticular-vein sclerotherapy; Nootheti et al. 2009 found that adding 3 weeks of 20–30 mmHg stockings after an initial week of stronger compression reduced pigmentation and bruising. PMIDs 17467226 and 19067733. The 2019 multidisciplinary compression guideline stated that after sclerotherapy clinicians should use best judgment because exact duration evidence remains limited. Walking soon after treatment is widely advised; strenuous exercise, heat and sun are commonly deferred for a short period.

### Adverse effects, contraindications and counseling

For sclerotherapy, common adverse effects: hyperpigmentation, bruising, injection pain, superficial thrombophlebitis/trapped blood and telangiectatic matting. ESVS notes transient hyperpigmentation, neovascularization and injection-site scar as common mild events. Cochrane found laser may cause less hyperpigmentation than sclerotherapy, while foam may cause more matting than liquid.

Serious uncommon sclerotherapy complications: skin necrosis/ulceration, often related to extravasation or inadvertent arterial injection; allergic reactions; thrombophlebitis; DVT; rarely pulmonary embolism; visual or neurologic symptoms, especially after foam. European guideline warns severe injection pain and immediate skin blanching are red flags; some post-foam visual symptoms may correspond to migraine with aura rather than stroke, but neurologic symptoms still require caution.

International Union of Phlebology 2023 consensus: major absolute contraindications include known hypersensitivity to sclerosant, acute VTE, severe neurologic or cardiac adverse events after previous sclerotherapy, severe acute systemic illness or infection, and critical limb ischemia. Relative contraindications include pregnancy/postpartum/breastfeeding, hypercoagulable states, risk of neurologic/cardiac adverse events, and poorly controlled chronic systemic illness.

For transcutaneous laser: tradeoffs are usually more pain during treatment and risks of burns, blistering, crusting, hyper/hypopigmentation and scarring if settings or skin selection are poor. ESLD guidance stresses parameters must match vessel size, depth and skin type. Sun-tanned skin increases risk of burns, blistering and dyspigmentation.

Red flags after treatment: persistent dark staining or matting are usually aesthetic, but sudden calf swelling, chest pain, breathlessness, major neurologic symptoms or a cold painful discolored foot require urgent assessment. After laser, persistent blistering, severe disproportionate pain, expanding redness or delayed ulceration require prompt review.

### Lipedema-specific context

Symptoms and visible vein treatment outcomes may diverge. Lipedema can coexist with chronic venous disease, but diffuse leg pain, tenderness, sensitivity and swelling are not automatically caused by visible spider veins. A 2024 study in patients with knee osteoarthritis found coexisting venous insufficiency or lipedema worsened pain, function and quality of life compared with OA alone. PMID 38092367.

Comorbidity appears common. In a retrospective vein-clinic study of 464 patients consulting for mild-to-moderate superficial venous disorder symptoms, 7.7% had lipedema. PMID 37422328; DOI 10.1016/j.jdmv.2023.06.002. Ultrasound lipedema studies routinely perform venous mapping; one study analyzed 89 women, including 63 with lipedema, using venous Doppler plus tissue-thickness measures. PMID 33853452; DOI 10.1177/02683555211002340.

Treating venous disease does not automatically fix lipedema symptoms. An OnlineFirst 2026 study of patients with both varicose veins and lipedema reported that lipedema symptoms were not influenced by endothermal ablation, with limitations including sample size and single-country setting. DOI 10.1177/02683555261418968.

Easy bruising/fragile capillaries require cautious wording. Easy bruising is often reported in lipedema, but is not pathognomonic and has many causes. 2024 German lipedema guideline cites small physiotherapy studies showing more petechiae and altered capillary fragility measures in lipedema, but those do not prove laser/sclerotherapy are inherently less safe in lipedema.

No strong direct evidence was found that lipedema itself increases post-sclerotherapy necrosis, DVT, hyperpigmentation or laser burns. No direct evidence was found that lipedema requires a different laser wavelength or automatically rules out sclerotherapy. The reasonable approach is cautious workup and expectation-setting: confirm whether venous reflux coexists, decide whether visible veins are the true treatment target, and warn that global pain, edema and tenderness may improve only partially or not at all.

Patient/community commentary: credible patient organizations and expert blogs reflect overlap between vein disease and lipedema. Lipedema.net tells patients to assess vein health first because symptoms overlap; the International Lipoedema Association argues against treating easy bruising as a reliable diagnostic sign. These are communication leads, not procedure-outcome evidence.

### Suggested Spanish patient-facing recommendations

- Las arañas vasculares o telangiectasias son venitas muy finas, superficiales, rojas/violetas/azules. Las venas reticulares son algo más profundas y gruesas, verdosas/azuladas. Las varices son más grandes, tortuosas y a veces abultadas.
- En piernas, la técnica con más respaldo suele ser escleroterapia para venas reticulares y muchas arañas vasculares. El láser transdérmico puede encajar cuando las venitas son muy finas, hay alergia al esclerosante, miedo a agujas, matting previo o escleroterapia fallida.
- La expectativa realista es mejoría parcial en varias sesiones, con posibilidad de recidiva, sobre todo en telangiectasias.
- Si hay dolor, ardor, pesadez, edema, venas abultadas, sangrado, tratamientos previos, sospecha de insuficiencia venosa o patrones que sugieran reflujo, conviene plantear eco-Doppler antes.
- En lipedema, tratar venas visibles puede mejorar un problema concreto, pero no cura el lipedema y puede no cambiar dolor difuso, sensibilidad cutánea o hinchazón global.
- Tras escleroterapia suele recomendarse compresión durante días/semanas, caminar y evitar esfuerzos intensos, calor intenso y sol directo. Algunos estudios apoyan hasta 3 semanas en venas pequeñas; la duración exacta depende del contexto y tolerancia.
- Buscar valoración médica si aparece dolor muy intenso, pie frío/pálido, ampollas importantes, úlcera, empeoramiento rápido del enrojecimiento, fiebre, hinchazón marcada de pantorrilla, falta de aire, dolor torácico o síntomas visuales/neurológicos persistentes.

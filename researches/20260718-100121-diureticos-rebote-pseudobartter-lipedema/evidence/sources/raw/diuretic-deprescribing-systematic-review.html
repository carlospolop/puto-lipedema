Abstract

In this systematic review, we report on the effects of diuretic deprescribing compared to continued diuretic use. We included clinical studies reporting on outcomes such as mortality, heart failure recurrence, tolerability and feasibility. We assessed risk of bias and certainty of the evidence using the GRADE framework. We included 25 publications from 22 primary studies (15 randomized controlled trials; 7 nonrandomized studies). The mean number of participants in the deprescribing groups was 35, and median/mean age 64 years. In patients with heart failure, there was no clear evidence that diuretic deprescribing was associated with increased mortality compared to diuretic continuation (low certainty evidence). The risk of cardiovascular composite outcomes associated with diuretic deprescribing was inconsistent (studies showing lower risk for diuretic deprescribing, or comparable risk with diuretic continuation; very low certainty evidence). The effect on heart failure recurrence after diuretic deprescribing in patients with diuretics for heart failure, and of hypertension in patients with diuretics for hypertension was inconsistent across the included studies (low certainty evidence). In patients with diuretics for hypertension, diuretic deprescribing was well tolerated (moderate certainty evidence), while in patients with diuretics for heart failure, deprescribing diuretics can result in complaints of peripheral oedema (very low certainty evidence). The overall risk of bias was generally high. In summary, this systematic review suggests that diuretic discontinuation could be a safe and feasible treatment option for carefully selected patients. However, there isa lack of high‐quality evidence on its feasibility, safety and tolerability of diuretic deprescribing, warranting further research.

Keywords: deprescribing, diuretics, heart failure, hypertension, patient‐relevant outcomes, systematic review

Abstract

1. INTRODUCTION

Diuretics have been a fundamental component in the treatment of heart failure (HF) and hypertension (HT) for several decades. 1 , 2 Despite their established efficacy in treating acute decompensated HF, chronic and prolonged diuretic use in the absence of fluid retention is not supported by high‐quality evidence, and is associated with potentially life‐threatening adverse events, 3 , 4 , 5 especially in older patients. 6 Notably, diuretics consistently rank among the drug classes with the highest rates of medication‐related hospital admissions. 7 , 8 , 9 Continuation of cardiovascular medications when no longer necessary or posing greater potential risks vs. benefits may adversely affect quality of life (QoL), induce adverse drug reactions (ADRs), escalate healthcare costs and increase mortality. 10 Further, chronic diuretic use may impede the guideline‐directed up‐titration of evidence‐based medications 11 , 12 and contribute to neurohumoral hyperactivation, particularly in older patients living with frailty. 13 Observational data suggest that prognosis in HF patients without chronically using a loop diuretic is generally good and comparable to those receiving low or standard doses of diuretics. 13 , 14 As such, reassessment of the benefits and harms of diuretics is good practice, and may bring up consideration of dose reduction or stopping of diuretics (deprescribing).

Since 2016, the European Society of Cardiology guidelines, 15 advocate for the de‐escalation of diuretics in stable HF patients. In addition, deprescribing antihypertensives is recommended in older and frail individuals with symptomatic low blood pressure (BP). 16 Despite these recommendations, diuretics are frequently overused and excessively dosed. 17 , 18 However, there are good reasons to deprescribe diuretics; for example, if there is no valid indication (anymore) for diuretic use, or if the diuretic is prescribed as part of a prescribing cascade. 19 In these settings, deprescribing should be considered. In addition, diuretic deprescribing may be appropriate if alternatives with more favourable benefit/risk ratios are available. The imperative to reassess the appropriateness of prolonged diuretic therapy and contemplate diuretic deprescribing is particularly pertinent in the context of HF, given the advent of innovative drugs such as sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT‐2) inhibitors and angiotensin receptor neprilysin inhibitors. 20 , 21 , 22 , 23

In routine practice, however, deprescribing remains underutilized, as evidenced by the limited implementation of guideline‐recommended loop diuretic dose reductions in specialized HF clinics across 33 European and European Society of Cardiology‐affiliated countries. 24 Various barriers to deprescribing related to patients, healthcare providers and healthcare systems have been identified, 25 which includes uncertainties in the benefits and harms of stopping or reducing medications compared to continuing. Guidelines and tools have been proposed as strategies to increase uptake of deprescribing in practice. To support healthcare providers in judicious decision‐making regarding when and how to safely taper or withdraw diuretics, our research group is presently developing an evidence‐based diuretic deprescribing clinical practice guideline. To inform the guideline, we conducted a systematic review to assess the advantages and drawbacks of diuretic deprescribing in comparison to the continued use of diuretics, and the feasibility of diuretic deprescribing in clinical practice.

2. METHODS

This systematic review was registered with PROSPERO, the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (CRD42022371658) and reported according to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic reviews and Meta‐Analyses‐network meta‐analysis (PRISMA) 2020 guidelines 26 (Table S1).

2.1. Literature search

The search strategy was developed in consultation with an experienced medical librarian. We searched Ovid Medline, Embase and the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews (via Cochrane Library). Key concepts were (i) diuretics and (ii) deprescribing. The search covered studies from inception of each database until 16 December 2022. We updated the search on 28 May 2024. We employed a backward snowballing approach from the reference lists of the included publications to identify potential additional relevant studies. The initial search strategy, and the search update are presented in Tables S2 and S3.

We imported the references identified through the search into EndNote X9 reference management software (Clarivate) and deduplicated them. Next, we exported the references to the Covidence program to facilitate the next steps in the systematic review process (Covidence systematic review software, Veritas Health Innovation, Melbourne, Australia. Available at www.covidence.org).

2.2. Criteria for considering studies for the review

We considered studies for inclusion if they reported effects of a diuretic deprescribing intervention, irrespective of study population, reasons for diuretic use or study follow‐up duration. We excluded studies published in non‐English language and studies reporting on deprescribing diuretic in temporary and/or acute conditions (e.g., perioperatively). Our outcomes of interest included, but were not limited to all‐cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, cardiovascular events (e.g., myocardial infarction, stroke), adverse diuretic withdrawal events (e.g., recurrence of HF or HT), health‐related QoL, and improvement/resolution of medication side effects or pill burden after diuretic withdrawal. For all in‐ and exclusion criteria, see Table 1.

2.3. Data collection and analysis

2.3.1. Selection of studies

After training sessions of the reviewer team by the project leader (E.v.P.) and piloting the title/abstract screening and full‐text review processes for refinement, title/abstract screening and full‐text review were performed by 2 independent reviewers (E.v.P., L.P., E.P., S.Ö., G.B.). The 2 independent reviewers discussed their conflicts until reaching consensus. When consensus was not reached, a third reviewer (N.v.d.V.) was involved in the discussion.

2.3.2. Data collection and management

Relevant data from the included publications were extracted into a data collection form by 2 independent reviewers (E.v.P., L.P., E.P., S.Ö., G.B.). Data collection included information on the study characteristics, diuretic deprescribing approach, primary and secondary outcomes. The 2 independent reviewers discussed their conflicts until reaching consensus. When consensus was not reached, a third reviewer (N.v.d.V.) was involved in the discussion.

2.3.3. Data synthesis and measures of the treatment effect

We focused on outcomes that we deemed are most important to patients, such as HF‐related hospital admissions, all‐cause mortality and QoL. Due to considerable heterogeneity in study design, patient characteristics, deprescribing interventions and/or outcomes reporting, it was not appropriate to combine the studies into a meta‐analysis. We narratively summarized the data by grouping outcome data based on patient population/condition: (i) adults taking diuretics for HF; (ii) adults taking diuretics for HT; and (iii) adults taking diuretics for other (e.g., peripheral oedema) or not specified indications. We did not perform subgroup or sensitivity analyses due to the limited number of studies for each outcome.

In addition, we narratively summarized the effects of diuretic deprescribing on other clinical outcomes (including effects on postprandial hypotension), biochemistry and cardiac ultrasonography measurements, and on the success‐rate of diuretic deprescribing attempts. Moreover, we narratively summarized the patient characteristics associated with successful diuretic deprescribing attempts.

2.3.4. Risk of bias assessment of included studies and certainty of evidence

The assessment of the quality of eligible studies was conducted by 2 independent reviewers (W.T., L.M., V.V.) using the revised Cochrane Risk of Bias (RoB 2) tool 27 for randomized trials, and the Risk Of Bias In Non‐Randomized Studies—of Interventions (ROBINS‐I) tool 28 for nonrandomized trials and observational studies having a control group. Each potential source of bias was ranked as high, low or unclear, with quotes provided from the study to justify the bias grade. We used RoB only as a descriptive measure (we did not exclude eligible studies based on the RoB assessment). Disagreements in scoring were resolved by discussion between the 2 independent reviewers, and further discussed with a third independent reviewer (E.v.P.) to reach consensus. We visualized the results using the RoBVis web application (https://github.com/mcguinlu/robvis). We assessed certainty of the evidence based on guidance provided in the GRADE (Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, and Evaluations) handbook (https://gdt.gradepro.org/app/handbook/handbook.html) and an evidence‐based medicine primer article. 29

3. RESULTS

3.1. Description of studies

A total of 7449 unique records were retrieved from databases and other sources (PRISMA flow‐chart, Figure 1). We reviewed 78 full text articles for eligibility. We included 25 reports from 22 studies. The details of the included studies are summarized in Table S4. The studies were published between 1977 and 2023. Deprescribing approaches included complete withdrawal and/or dose reduction. Fourteen trials were conducted in Europe, 6 in the Americas (North America, South America, Central America, Caribbean) and 2 in Asia Pacific (Central and South Asia, Northeastern Asia, Southeastern Asia, Australia, and Oceania). Fifteen studies were randomized controlled trials; 7 were nonrandomized studies. Study follow‐up duration ranged from 2 weeks to 6 years. Most studies (10/22 = 45%) included participants with HF, 27% (6/22) with HT, 9% (2/22) peripheral oedema and 18% (4/22) included participants with diuretics for HF, HT, peripheral oedema and/or unknown reasons. The mean number of participants in the diuretic deprescribing groups was 35 (range 13–671). The median/mean age of the participants in the diuretic deprescribing groups was 64 years (range 56–82 years), and the median percentage of females was 36% (range 0–88%). Fifteen studies were in outpatient hospital populations, 1 in hospital in‐ or outpatients, 4 in primary care populations, and 2 studies were in a nursing home population.

3.2. Risk of bias in included studies

The results of the critical appraisal of the outcomes in the included studies are shown in Tables 2 and 3. The overall RoB of outcomes (Figures 2 and 3) in the randomized trials was high in 10/18 (56%) studies. There were some concerns in 1 (6%) and in low concern in 7/18 (39%) studies. The overall RoB in the nonrandomized studies was critical in 1 study (1/7 = 20%), serious in 4 studies (57%) and moderate for 2 studies (29%). The RoB assessment was based on the analysis of the primary outcome for each study; analyses of both primary and secondary outcomes were included in the summary of findings analyses (Tables 4, 5, 6).

3.3. Outcomes

3.3.1. Effects of diuretic deprescribing in patients using diuretics for HF

In studies among patients with diuretics for HF (Table 4), there were inconsistent results regarding the effect on mortality for diuretic deprescribing vs. continuation (low certainty evidence): in 1 trial, 30 (cardiovascular) mortality was lower in the diuretic deprescribing group compared to the diuretic continuation group. In a large European registry cohort, there was a trend toward lower cardiovascular mortality in the diuretic deprescribing group compared to the stable dose group, whereas the risk of HF‐related mortality was comparable between both groups. 24 In another trial, 31 mortality rates were comparable between the diuretic deprescribing and continuation groups. In none of the studies, mortality was higher in diuretic deprescribing groups compared to diuretic continuation groups. In all other trials, there were no deaths in the deprescribing nor in the continuation groups. In 3 studies, the primary endpoint was a composite including (cardiac and all‐cause) mortality combined with HF‐related hospitalization and/or heart transplantation or durable ventricular assist device implantation. 32 , 33 , 34 In the 2 studies among patients with stable HF with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), 32 , 33 diuretic deprescribing was associated with (borderline) significantly better outcomes than the control intervention: 25% in the continuation group vs. none in the deprescribing group experienced cardiac death or HF‐related hospitalization at 1 year of follow‐up; P = .047), and at 2 years 55% for the continuation group vs. 25% in the deprescribing group (P = .053). 32 In the other trial, patients in the diuretic down‐titration group had a lower risk for the composite clinical end point of HF readmissions and all‐cause mortality compared to patients in the unchanged dose or uptitration group (adjusted hazard ratio 0.43; confidence interval [CI] 0.22 to 0.83; P = .012). 33 Likewise, in patients with dilated cardiomyopathy, loop diuretic withdrawal was associated with a reduced risk of the composite outcome of all‐cause mortality, heart transplantation, durable ventricular‐assist device implantation and HF hospitalization (HR 0.37; 95% CI 0.29–0.48; P < .001).34 However, in a large European registry study, the risk of a composite outcome (all‐cause mortality or HF hospitalization) was comparable between the diuretic deprescribing and stable dose groups (17.7 and 19%, respectively).24

The effects on treatment emergent recurrent HF episodes for diuretic deprescribing vs. continuation was inconsistent (low certainty evidence): recurrent congestive HF episodes after deprescribing was observed in 6 trials, occurring in 10–58% of patients. 31 , 35 , 36 , 37 , 38 , 39 In 2 of the trials, (treatment emergent) HF episodes were significantly more frequent in the deprescribing group compared to the control group. 35 , 39 In 4 other trials, the frequencies between groups were comparable between both groups. 36 , 37 , 38 , 40

In 3 reports of a single trial population, an increase in BP was observed after deprescribing diuretics (very low certainty evidence), occurring in 2–10% of participants. 31 , 36 , 37 , 38 In 2 of these reports, ankle oedema was observed after diuretic deprescribing (low certainty evidence), occurring in 6 and 11% of the patients, respectively. 31 , 36

In both studies reporting on HF symptoms as the primary outcome, effects on Kansas City Cardiomyopathy Questionnaire, 6‐min walking distance and/or patient‐reported assessment of dyspnoea based on a visual analogue scale were comparable between the diuretic deprescribing and continuation groups (P = .94; low certainty evidence). 38 , 41

In the only study reporting on tolerability of the deprescribing attempt as primary outcome in older adults with HF with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF), 42 10% of participants restarted the diuretic on their explicit request because of peripheral oedema, suggesting that diuretic deprescribing may not be tolerated well in this population (very low certainty evidence). In both studies reporting on the tolerability of the deprescribing intervention (secondary outcomes), there was evidence of an increased risk of adverse effects following diuretic deprescribing. In 1 study, 43 10% (2/20) of participants were not willing to continue the deprescribing intervention, but details were not provided. In 2 trials, patient feelings of well‐being and the frequency of side effects between diuretic deprescribing groups and control groups were reported. 44 , 45 In both trials, there were no significant differences between both groups. One study reported that patients after spironolactone withdrawal experienced more HF symptom aggravation than in the continuation group. 39 A recent double‐blind, multicentre, randomized trial on short‐term diuretic withdrawal in stable outpatients with mild HF and no fluid retention receiving optimal therapy reported adverse events in detail. The frequency of most common reported complaints (dyspnoea/tiredness, and dizziness or symptoms related to hypotension) were comparable between the diuretic deprescribing and continuation groups. Facial and limb oedema were reported by 7 patients in the withdrawal group vs. none of the continuation group. 38

In 2 separate publications by a Dutch research group, 37 , 42 diuretic deprescribing was associated with statistically significant improvements in BP homeostasis on active standing (n = 21 participants; P < .001), and in postprandial hypotension compared to diuretic continuation (n = 13 participants; P = .01).

The percentage of adults in whom the diuretic deprescribing attempt was successful at last follow‐up ranged from 5 41 to 100%. 30 The most reported reason for failure of the deprescribing attempt was recurrence of HF or HT. Incidentally, diuretics were reinitiated for peripheral oedema.

3.3.2. Effects of diuretic deprescribing in patients using diuretics for hypertension

In 4 trials among patients using diuretics for HT 44 , 45 , 46 , 47 (Table 5), effects on recurrence of HT after diuretic withdrawal were conflicting (low certainty evidence): in 2 studies, the percentage of patients with treatment emergent HT was higher in the diuretic deprescribing groups compared to the diuretic continuation groups. 44 , 47 In 2 other studies, the percentage of patients with controlled BP 45 and systolic or diastolic BPs 46 were comparable between both groups.

A posthoc analysis of the SPRINT (Systolic blood PRessure INtervention) trial 48 demonstrated that diuretic deprescribing is associated with an increased risk of acute decompensated HF episodes (4% of the patients in the standard treatment arm, vs. 1% in the intensive treatment arm; low certainty evidence). In a randomized study among older adults with mild cognitive impairment, diuretic deprescribing had no favourable effect on reducing orthostatic hypotension compared to diuretic continuation (risk ratio 1.27; 95% CI 0.85 to 1.89; P = .25; moderate certainty evidence). 49 In both studies evaluating patient feelings of well‐being and the frequency of side effects between diuretic deprescribing and control interventions, no differences were observed between both groups, 44 , 45 demonstrating that the deprescribing intervention is well‐tolerated (moderate certainty evidence). The percentage of individuals who remained free of diuretics until last follow‐up after diuretic withdrawal in HT trials ranged from 23 to 99%. If treatment‐emergent HT occurred, this was generally within 3 months of deprescribing.

3.3.3. Effects of diuretic deprescribing in patients using diuretics for indications other than HF or HT, or for nonspecified indications

In 3 trials among patients using diuretics for indications other than HF and HT, or for nonspecified indications 50 , 51 , 52 (Table 6), no effect of diuretic deprescribing on mortality was observed (very low certainty evidence). The effect of diuretic deprescribing on ankle oedema was inconsistent between the studies 50 , 51 , 52 (very low certainty evidence). The only study reporting on patient well‐being after diuretic deprescribing 50 demonstrated that this intervention may not be tolerated well diuretic deprescribing (very low certainty evidence): 3% reported feeling unwell and complained of urinary incontinence.

In a retrospective cohort study among individuals who were admitted to a long‐term care facility, 31 diuretics (irrespective of indication) were successfully withdrawn up to 1 year of follow‐up in 58% of the population. In case diuretic withdrawal was not feasible, this was apparent within the first 3 months after withdrawal attempt in the majority (88%) of individuals, and was related to HF (n = 26, 76%), ankle oedema (n = 5, 15%), HT (n = 2, 6%) and unclear (n = 1, 3%). In this study, diuretics were successfully withdrawn in almost half (49%) of individuals who had used diuretics for a previous diagnosis of HF. The authors calculated that the probability of remaining free from diuretics for at least 1 year in individuals using diuretics for an unknown indication was 0.53.

3.3.4. Effects of diuretic deprescribing on biochemical and cardiac ultrasonography measurements

The majority (4/5) of included trials demonstrated that diuretic deprescribing was associated with better renal function tests (e.g., serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen) compared to patients who continued using diuretics. 30 , 32 , 42 , 47 , 53 In a randomized study in stable HFrEF patients, renal function worsened in 2/18 (11%) of the patients in the deprescribing group vs. 12/18 (67%) of patients in the continuation group (P = .005). 32 One of these publications reported a significant increase in creatinine clearance in individuals in whom diuretics were withdrawn successfully (from 63 ± 3 mL/min to 69 ± 4 mL/min; P = .01). 42 However, a more recent publication, performed in a cohort of stable HF patients receiving optimal HF therapy did not confirm a beneficial effect of diuretic deprescribing on renal function (creatinine and blood urea nitrogen levels were comparable between both groups). 38

In publications on a trial in patients with HT, withdrawal of diuretics significantly reduced plasma renin activity, while not affecting plasma aldosterone, norepinephrine and epinephrine levels compared with continued diuretic use. 47 , 53 A study in older HFpEF patients showed that successful diuretic deprescribing was associated with decreased renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system activation, an effect persisting until last follow‐up at 3 months. 36

The included studies consistently showed that fasting or postprandial serum glucose, serum uric acid and lipid profiles were comparable in diuretic deprescribing and continuation groups. 44 , 47 , 53 A recent publication demonstrated that median change in levels of N‐terminal pro‐brain natriuretic peptide (NT‐pro‐BNP) was comparable between diuretic deprescribing and continuation groups. 38

Few included trials evaluated the effect of diuretic deprescribing compared to diuretic continuation on cardiac ultrasonography measurements. One study reported no difference in left ventricular hypertrophy. 47 Another study demonstrated a significant increase in early/late atrial ventricular filling velocity in individuals in whom diuretics were withdrawn successfully (from 0.696 ± 0.05 to 0.796 ± 0.06; P < .02). 42 In a retrospective study in a cohort of HFrEF patients, no echocardiographic parameters worsened after diuretic withdrawal at regular follow‐up during a median follow‐up duration of 24 ± 9 months. 30 In a cohort of older people with HFpEF, diuretic deprescribing was shown to improve postprandial hypotension: in the withdrawal group, maximum postprandial declines in stroke volume and cardiac output decreased from −9 ± 1 to −4 ± 2 mL (P = .01), and from −0.6 ± 0.2 to −0.2 ± 0.1 L/min (P = .04), respectively. 37

3.3.5. Patient characteristics associated with successful diuretic deprescribing attempts

In 2 Dutch studies aiming to evaluate diuretic deprescribing possibilities, 1 in a general practice 35 and the other in a nursing home population 31 found that successful diuretic deprescribing is linked to the absence of a valid indication for diuretic use or a history/diagnosis of HF.

Romano et al. stated that diuretic withdrawal was safe in patients with HFrEF and NT‐pro‐BNP values <550 pg/mL and tricuspid annular plane systolic excursion >20 mm and/or left ventricular end‐diastolic diameter <60 mm on echocardiography, regardless of their baseline diuretic dose. 30 Rohde et al. 38 found that successful diuretic attempts in stable mild HF patients were probably relatively young, having mild and stable symptoms, and no or minimal clinical signs of congestion. There was a trend to unsuccessful deprescribing if the baseline furosemide dose was 80 mg compared to those on 40 mg. Baseline NT‐pro‐BNP with a cut‐off of 652 pg/mL appeared not predictive of the success of the deprescribing attempt. In patients with HFrEF after cardiac resynchronization therapy. Loop diuretic deprescribing was most likely in patients with nonischaemic cardiomyopathy, higher baseline diuretic dose, higher ejection fraction and lower right ventricular systolic pressure at 6 weeks. 33

A large European multicentre registry study 24 demonstrated that in patients with chronic HF, higher systolic BP (OR per 10 mmHg change 1.11; 95% CI 1.01 to 1.22; P = .032), absence of sleep apnoea (OR 0.24, 95% CI 0.09 to 0.69; P = .008), absence of moderate/severe mitral valve regurgitation (OR 0.57; 95% CI 0.37 to 0.87; P = .008) and absence of peripheral congestion (OR 0.48; 95% CI 0.29 to 0.80; P = .005) were associated with successful loop diuretic dose reduction. In an Italian registry study among outpatients with dilated cardiomyopathy, 34 younger age, absence of moderate to severe mitral regurgitation and higher doses of β‐blockers were independently associated with stopping loop diuretic therapy within 24 months in multivariate analysis. Compared to those who had successfully withdrawn their loop diuretic, those who continued loop diuretics were older, had a lower left ventricular ejection fraction, a larger left atrium and were more likely to have moderate–severe mitral regurgitation, a restrictive filling pattern and atrial fibrillation. 34

In a study in patients with mild HT, lower pre‐treatment systolic and diastolic BP values predicted diuretic deprescribing success. 44 In another study in patients with HT, lower diastolic BP at baseline and medication compliance were linked to successful diuretic deprescribing (P = .003 and P = .053, respectively). 45

4. DISCUSSION

This is the first systematic review to investigate the benefits and harms of diuretic deprescribing. Our findings underscore the paucity of high‐quality evidence on the feasibility, safety and tolerability of diuretic deprescribing: most studies included in this review exhibited limitations such as small sample sizes, short follow‐up durations and overall low to moderate quality, with a heightened susceptibility to bias. Heterogeneity among the trials was substantial. Although based on data from a limited set of clinical trials with a low grade of evidence, we found that diuretic therapy discontinuation could be a safe and feasible treatment option that deserves consideration for carefully selected patients. In patients with HF, mortality risk associated with diuretic deprescribing was comparable to the risk with diuretic continuation (low certainty evidence). The risk of cardiovascular composite outcomes including mortality associated with diuretic deprescribing was inconsistent, with studies showing lower risk for diuretic deprescribing, or comparable with diuretic continuation (very low certainty evidence). The effect on recurrence of HF after diuretic deprescribing in patients with diuretics for HF, and of HT in patients with diuretics for HT was inconsistent across the included studies (low certainty evidence). In patients with diuretics for HT, diuretic deprescribing was well tolerated (moderate certainty evidence), while in patients with diuretics for HF, deprescribing diuretics can result in complaints of peripheral oedema (very low certainty evidence). In patients with diuretics for HT, however, the risk of developing peripheral oedema was comparable in the diuretic deprescribing compared to the continuation group (moderate quality evidence).

Across the studies included in this review, the feasibility of diuretic deprescribing varied considerably with success percentages ranging from 5 to 100%. Patient characteristics associated with successful deprescribing included the absence of valid diuretic indications or a history/diagnosis of HF, and left ventricular ejection fraction deprescribing failures were mostly attributed to (recurrent) congestive HF episodes or HT recurrence. Mortality outcomes in HF trials showed inconsistent results, which is in line with the findings of several reviews on the effects of deprescribing of medications. 54 , 55 Data from the included studies suggested that diuretic deprescribing compared to diuretic continuation improves renal function. Biochemical (cardiometabolic and neurohumoral biomarkers) and cardiac ultrasonography measurements were comparable between diuretic deprescribing and continuation groups. Tolerability was generally acceptable, but the available evidence pertaining to the tolerability and effects on well‐being or QoL associated with diuretic deprescribing was notably limited. This discrepancy is noteworthy given that deprescribing inherently embodies a patient‐centred approach, with the explicit goal of optimizing pharmacotherapy to enhance patient outcomes. 56

There has been a limited number of prior systematic reviews investigating the safety and feasibility of antihypertensive withdrawal including diuretics. One such review, encompassing 66 publications, indicated that approximately 25% of individuals using antihypertensives could safely discontinue the medication without experiencing a recurrence of HT. 57 Notably, systematic reviews focusing on older adults observed an increase in systolic and diastolic BP following withdrawal, but no statistically significant difference in mortality. 54 , 58

The evidence derived from the data included in our review has several limitations. First, we judged the certainty of the evidence to be low or very low for all outcomes considered. Therefore, we are uncertain in the overall evidence on diuretic deprescribing. Methodological and reporting limitations were prevalent across the majority of included studies, and unexpected observations in randomized studies raised concerns about small study effects, selection bias and publication bias. Because there were too few studies to establish asymmetry visually in funnel plots, we were not able to assess publication bias. We downgraded certainty primarily due to the RoB related to imprecision, inconsistency and indirectness. Furthermore, it is challenging to determine the applicability of our findings to contemporary clinical practice: the majority of the HF trials were performed decades ago in relatively healthy, young and predominantly male HFrEF patients. In contrast, contemporary real‐world HF populations are characterized by patients who are predominantly older, frail and multimorbid, 59 with a large proportion of female patients with HFpEF. 60 However, none of the trials included in our review measured or reported frailty, and only 1 nonrandomized study was performed in a nursing home population, 31 with residents who were likely to be frail. Frailty is associated with an increased risk of adverse outcomes and ADRs in older HF patients. 61 The paucity of studies underscores the exclusion of vulnerable populations from HF clinical trials. 61 The association between frailty and adverse outcomes, coupled with the increased risk of ADRs in older HF patients, underscores the importance of considering frailty status in diuretic deprescribing decision‐making. 13 Based on their increased risk of both acute decompensated HF episodes and adverse drug events in pre‐frail and frail individuals (Clinical Frailty Scale ≥ 4), the decision to deprescribe should be weighed carefully and repeatedly over time, and with greater vigilance compared to individuals with a lower Clinical Frailty Scale score. In addition, it should be emphasized that HF standards of care at time of the majority of studies were of notoriously lower quality compared to contemporary care, characterized by evidence‐based disease‐modifying drugs and multidisciplinary HF outpatient programmes. 62 Literature suggests that patients treated for HF according to the current guidelines are less likely to develop HF after deprescribing diuretics. 63 , 64

There are limitations to our review process. First, the term deprescribing is relatively new, 65 even though the concept and clinical practice have existed for a long time. Additionally, given the rapid evolution in the field, we may have missed potentially relevant studies. By developing a comprehensive literature search by an experienced medical librarian and using a variety of different search terms for deprescribing/withdrawal, however, we mitigated this risk. Second, it is important to consider that we excluded observational studies reporting on the effects of diuretic deprescribing in individuals both before and after deprescribing. Data from these observations may be valuable for real‐world clinical practice. By contrast, the scope of our review was broad (we did not exclude based on the indication for diuretic use), aligning with real‐life clinical practice needs. Third, we excluded studies in which diuretics were deprescribed after initiating a medication from another drug class, for example SGLT‐2 inhibitors. In the currently rapidly evolving landscape of HF treatment, with the newer medication classes such as angiotensin receptor neprilysin inhibitors and SGLT‐2 inhibitors, this is a shortcoming. In addition, we grouped the studies based on the indication for diuretic use (HF, HT and peripheral oedema). The reason for this approach was pragmatic, and to facilitate data synthesis. However, we realize that it is likely that 1 or more indicator conditions occurred concomitantly in individuals included in the trials. The strengths of our systematic review process include the methodological rigour and the comprehensive literature search. Search limitations of animal and non‐English studies were performed manually rather than relying on automatic limitations to reduce retrieval loss.

This systematic review has several implications for clinical practice and future research. With regard to clinical practice, we intend to incorporate the findings of this review into an evidence‐based diuretic deprescribing clinical practice guideline that we are currently developing. While recognizing the imperfect nature of the available evidence from this systematic review, our findings compile the best available evidence on the clinical effects of diuretic deprescribing. With this clinical practice guideline, we aim to support healthcare providers in judicious decision‐making regarding when and how to safely taper or withdraw diuretics. In terms of future research, our review underscores the need for well‐designed clinical trials targeting representative contemporary patient populations. The scarcity of literature on diuretic deprescribing in older, frail individuals warrants future studies incorporating frailty subgroups. Defining a core outcome set for diuretic deprescribing studies, including frailty assessment and relevant outcomes such as falls, urinary incontinence, and functionality will enhance result synthesis and contribute to a more substantial body of evidence. Emphasis on patient‐centred outcomes, including preferences for communication about diuretic deprescribing proposals and changes in health‐related QoL, is crucial. 66 Moreover, investigations into the relationship between diuretic deprescribing and economic outcomes are needed.

5. CONCLUSIONS

In conclusion, our systematic review provides valuable insights into the challenges and opportunities associated with diuretic deprescribing. Although based on data from a limited set of clinical trials with a low grade of evidence, we conclude that diuretic therapy discontinuation could be a safe and feasible treatment option that deserves consideration for carefully selected patients: successful deprescribing attempts were associated with specific patient characteristics. Additionally, we demonstrated that the benefit/risk balance of diuretic deprescribing compared to continued use may vary dependent on the clinical setting. For example, the risk of recurrent HF was comparable between diuretic deprescribing and diuretic continuation in HF patients, whereas the risk was higher patients with HT who discontinued diuretics. This underscores the need for individualized diuretic deprescribing approaches. Nevertheless, there is a paucity of high‐certainty evidence applicable to current practice, and particularly in situations where deprescribing is often considered clinically, such as frailty and falls. There is also a lack of data on key outcomes crucial to older adults, including functional independence, 67 falls and QoL. Future research efforts are crucial to address these knowledge gaps, particularly in understanding the safety and potential harms of diuretic deprescribing in older and multimorbid adults. Standardization in future diuretic deprescribing interventions and consistent reporting of outcomes in relevant patient categories are essential to enhance our understanding and refine diuretic deprescribing in clinical practice.

In the meanwhile, the insights from this systematic review represent the best available evidence on clinical experience with diuretic deprescribing. As such, our findings will contribute to the formulation of diuretic deprescribing recommendations within the evidence‐based diuretic deprescribing clinical practice guideline currently under development by our group.

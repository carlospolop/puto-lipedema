# Round 2 subresearch: cross-condition genes for lymphatic/vascular overgrowth and connective-tissue phenotypes related to lipedema

**Scope.** This note maps genes/syndromes that reproduce *components* of lipedema biology: lymphatic dysfunction/edema, capillary leak/permeability, vascular malformations, fibro-adipose overgrowth, connective-tissue laxity, fibrosis, and easy bruising. It deliberately separates (1) **differential diagnoses / phenocopies** from (2) **plausible shared mechanisms or modifiers** in otherwise typical lipedema.

**Bottom line.** The strongest cross-condition convergence is not a single “lipedema gene” but three modules: **lymphatic development/flow** (FLT4/VEGFC/VEGFD/FOXC2/GJC2/PIEZO1/CCBE1/ADAMTS3/EPHB4/PROX1/SOX18), **PI3K-AKT-mTOR vascular-adipose overgrowth** (PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN/TEK), and **connective-tissue/ECM remodeling** (TNXB/COL5A1/COL5A2/COL3A1/ELN/FBN1 plus TGFB/MMP/LOX/FN1/POSTN). These pathways can explain edema load, tissue mechanics, bruising, fibrosis, and adipose expansion, but most are currently **differential or modifier candidates**, not established causes of classical bilateral female-predominant lipedema.

---

## 1) Anchor: what is already seen in lipedema tissue

Recent lipedema reviews and histology studies support a mixed vascular-lymphatic-ECM phenotype:

- Lipedema is clinically characterized by disproportionate lower-body subcutaneous adipose expansion, pain/tenderness, **easy bruising**, and relative foot sparing; it is often confused with obesity and lymphedema.
- Tissue work reports **dilated blood and lymphatic microvessels, angiogenesis, macrophage infiltration, adipocyte hypertrophy**, interstitial fluid, and fibrosis/remodeling.
- A 2026 vascular review summarizes a central interplay among **vascular leak/fragility, lymphatic handling, connective tissue, immune infiltration, adipose hypertrophy, and fibrosis**. It specifically notes chronic VEGF/VEGF-C signaling, endothelial junction changes, glycocalyx/ECM remodeling, and collagen/MMP imbalance.
- A 2025 capillary study found **capillary wall thickening, endothelial/pericyte hyperplasia, endothelial degeneration**, larger adipocytes, macrophage enrichment, and fibrosis measures in affected tissue.
- Family/exome work in lipedema identified variants in panel genes including **TNXB** and **STAB1**, and discussed ECM overrepresentation; this is suggestive but not causal proof.

Interpretation: lipedema has enough vascular/lymphatic/ECM signal to justify cross-condition gene review, but differential diagnosis must stay separate from lipedema mechanism.

---

## 2) Lymphatic development, lymph drainage, and edema genes

### Differential diagnosis role

These genes are classic **primary lymphedema / lymphatic malformation** genes. They should be prioritized when a patient labeled “lipedema” has pedal/toe edema, congenital/childhood onset, lymphangiectasia, recurrent cellulitis, asymmetric edema, hydrops history, distichiasis, telangiectasia, systemic malformations, or abnormal lymphoscintigraphy/ICG.

| Gene/pathway | Cross-condition phenotype | Why it overlaps lipedema | Why it is usually not classical lipedema |
|---|---|---|---|
| **FLT4 / VEGFR3** | Milroy/congenital primary lymphedema; lymphatic hypoplasia/dysfunction | Leg swelling, heaviness, tissue-fluid excess; lipedema tissue has reported FLT4/VEGFR3 axis changes | Milroy is often congenital/pedal and not primarily painful symmetric adipose hypertrophy |
| **VEGFC** | Milroy-like lymphedema; ligand for FLT4/VEGFR3 | VEGF-C can drive lymphangiogenesis and vascular permeability; serum VEGF-C is reported elevated in lipedema | VEGFC pathogenic variants cause primary lymphedema phenotypes, not validated lipedema |
| **VEGFD** | Lymphangiogenic/angiogenic ligand; less often a Mendelian PL gene | Local VEGF-D alterations could alter lymphatic compensation and vascular permeability | Better as pathway marker/modifier than diagnostic gene |
| **FOXC2** | Lymphedema-distichiasis; lymphatic and venous valve dysfunction, varicosities | Puberty/adult leg swelling, venous-lymphatic reflux, edema, heaviness | Distichiasis/varicosities/valve disease are syndromic clues; fat pain pattern differs |
| **GJC2 / connexin 47** | Hereditary primary lymphedema families | Gap-junction signaling may affect lymphatic coordination/flow | Explains edema more than adipose nodularity or easy bruising |
| **PIEZO1** | Generalized lymphatic dysplasia, nonimmune hydrops, primary lymphedema | Mechanosensitive flow/pressure signaling is highly relevant to edematous adipose tissue | Usually systemic/congenital lymphatic disease rather than isolated lipedema |
| **CCBE1** | Hennekam lymphangiectasia-lymphedema syndrome | Required for VEGF-C activation/maturation; lymphangiectasia and edema | Syndromic AR disease with intestinal/face/developmental features in many cases |
| **ADAMTS3** | Hennekam lymphangiectasia-lymphedema syndrome 3 | Protease that activates VEGF-C; connects ECM processing to lymphangiogenesis | Severe/syndromic lymphatic maldevelopment, not typical isolated lipedema |
| **EPHB4** | Lymphatic malformation / primary lymphedema; venous/lymphatic development | Fits mixed venous-lymphatic malformation and limb swelling | Usually malformation/lymphedema phenotype; not symmetric estrogen-linked adipose disease |
| **PROX1** | Master lymphatic endothelial identity; rare primary lymphedema candidates | If lymphatic endothelial identity/maintenance is impaired, edema and adipose remodeling can follow | In lipedema, PROX1 is mainly a pathway hypothesis; not established causal gene |
| **SOX18** | Hypotrichosis-lymphedema-telangiectasia; lymphatic/vascular development | Telangiectasia + edema + vascular fragility can mimic parts of lipedema | Hair/telangiectasia/syndromic clues distinguish it |

### Plausible shared mechanism in lipedema

- **Edema-adipose feedback:** impaired lymph transport or excess capillary filtration raises interstitial fluid/protein load, increasing macrophage recruitment, fibroblast activation, and ECM deposition; chronic edema can promote adipocyte hypertrophy/hyperplasia.
- **VEGF-C/VEGFR3 axis:** lipedema studies report elevated VEGF-C and altered VEGF/FLT4/TEK signaling. This may be compensatory lymphangiogenesis or a maladaptive permeability signal. It does **not** prove FLT4/VEGFC Mendelian lipedema.
- **Mechanotransduction:** PIEZO1-like biology is attractive because lipedema tissue has altered pressure, stretch, pain, and fluid mechanics; however, evidence is currently indirect.
- **ECM-lymph coupling:** CCBE1/ADAMTS3 show that extracellular matrix processing can gate VEGF-C activation. This is a useful conceptual bridge between fibrosis and lymphatic dysfunction.

**Use in study design:** include these genes in a *differential/phenotype-modifier tier* for lipedema cohorts, especially in “lipedema + objective lymphedema/early onset/family edema” subgroups. Do not count them as direct lipedema genes unless variant segregation plus typical phenotype is shown.

## 3) PI3K-AKT-mTOR, vascular malformations, and fibro-adipose overgrowth

### Differential diagnosis role

This group is the major phenocopy set for **regional adipose overgrowth + vascular/lymphatic malformations + pain/swelling**.

| Gene | Syndrome/condition | Overlap with lipedema | Distinguishing clues |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PIK3CA** | PIK3CA-related overgrowth spectrum (PROS): CLOVES, MCAP, Klippel-Trenaunay-like phenotypes, FAVA/macrodactyly | Lipomatous/fibro-adipose overgrowth, venous/lymphatic/capillary malformations, swelling, pain | Usually mosaic, congenital/childhood, segmental/asymmetric, epidermal nevi/skeletal anomalies; needs affected-tissue testing |
| **AKT1** | Proteus syndrome | Progressive connective-tissue/adipose overgrowth and vascular malformations | Mosaic, patchy, progressive deforming overgrowth; not symmetric female-predominant limb fat |
| **PTEN** | PTEN hamartoma tumor syndrome; Bannayan-Riley-Ruvalcaba; SOLAMEN if segmental second hit | Lipomas, vascular anomalies, tissue overgrowth; PI3K brake defect | Macrocephaly, mucocutaneous lesions, cancer risk, GI polyps/developmental features; often germline testing |
| **TEK / TIE2** | Inherited/sporadic venous malformations; blue rubber bleb nevus-like phenotypes; often somatic | Painful subcutaneous venous lesions, swelling, local tenderness; lipedema studies implicate Tie2/vascular stability | Blue/purple compressible lesions, localized VM on imaging, phleboliths, bleeding/thrombosis; not diffuse symmetric lipedema |

### Plausible shared mechanism in lipedema

- **Adipose + vascular co-expansion:** PIK3CA/AKT/PTEN disorders prove that activating growth signaling in local mesenchymal/endothelial compartments can produce adipose overgrowth with vascular/lymphatic anomalies. That makes PI3K-AKT-mTOR a plausible shared *module* for lipedema adipose expansion, but not necessarily a primary mosaic overgrowth syndrome.
- **Endothelial barrier and Tie2:** TEK/Tie2 normally stabilizes endothelial junctions. Lipedema tissue/secretome studies report endothelial junction/permeability changes (e.g., VE-cadherin/ZO-1 changes) and vascular review notes Tie2 relevance. Reduced vascular stability could increase capillary leak and bruising.
- **AKT1 as convergence, not diagnosis:** AKT1 appears in lipedema multi-omics/epigenetic context in prior notes, and it is central to insulin/growth/adipocyte survival. But somatic AKT1 Proteus syndrome is a differential diagnosis, not evidence that AKT1 causes classical lipedema.

**Use in study design:** if the phenotype is asymmetric, congenital, segmental, malformation-heavy, or includes macrodactyly/lipomatous masses, prioritize mosaic testing of affected tissue for **PIK3CA/AKT1/TEK** and germline **PTEN** as appropriate. In typical lipedema, analyze this pathway as expression/phosphoprotein signaling rather than assuming pathogenic variants.

## 4) Connective-tissue laxity, bruising, fascia/skin mechanics, and EDS-like genes

### Differential diagnosis and comorbidity role

Several connective-tissue disorders overlap with lipedema symptoms: easy bruising, pain, hypermobility, soft tissue fragility, edema, venous issues, and abnormal fascia/ECM mechanics.

| Gene | Disorder/phenotype | Overlap with lipedema | Distinguishing clues / caution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TNXB** | TNXB-related classical-like EDS: generalized joint hypermobility, hyperextensible skin, easy bruising, sometimes leg edema without cardiac failure | Strong overlap with bruising, laxity, tissue compliance, and female-predominant hypermobility comorbidity; lipedema family exome work found TNXB variants | TNXB causes an EDS subtype; not proven lipedema. Look for hyperextensible skin, joint instability, prolapse, neuropathy, family pattern |
| **COL5A1 / COL5A2** | Classical EDS | Easy bruising, skin/soft tissue fragility, pain, hypermobility | Atrophic/papyraceous scarring and skin hyperextensibility distinguish cEDS |
| **COL3A1** | Vascular EDS | Easy bruising, thin translucent skin, vascular/organ fragility | High-stakes differential. Arterial/intestinal/uterine rupture risk; do not label as benign bruising/lipedema if red flags present |
| **ELN** | Elastin disorders, supravalvular aortic stenosis/Williams-Beuren spectrum, cutis laxa-like vascular/skin elasticity phenotypes | Elastic recoil and vessel wall properties may influence bruising/venous pooling/tissue stretch | Syndromic cardiovascular/elastic tissue clues; lipedema panel paper listed ELN in syndromic fat-accumulation context |
| **FBN1** | Marfan syndrome / fibrillinopathy | Lax connective tissue, vascular risk, pain, long-limb habitus; possible venous/skin mechanical effects | Aortic root/ocular/skeletal criteria; not a lipedema gene |

### Plausible shared mechanism in lipedema

- **Fragile support matrix:** collagen V/III, tenascin-X, elastin, and fibrillin abnormalities can reduce tissue tensile strength and vessel support, plausibly increasing bruising, venous pooling, capillary fragility, and tissue stretch under adipose expansion.
- **Interstitial compliance:** looser ECM could allow more fluid accumulation and adipose expansion while also making lymphatic pumping less efficient. Lipedema interstitial-fluid work explicitly discusses dermal fiber spacing, collagen separation, ELN/TNXB-like elasticity, and hypermobile connective-tissue disorders as relevant hypotheses.
- **Pain and proprioceptive load:** hypermobility/EDS can add musculoskeletal pain, dysautonomia, venous pooling, and fatigue, confounding lipedema pain severity.

**Use in study design:** screen for hypermobility/skin/vascular red flags, but keep **comorbid connective-tissue disorder** separate from “lipedema causality.” TNXB is the most interesting bridge because it appears both in EDS biology and lipedema family-variant work.

## 5) Fibrosis, ECM remodeling, stiffness, and adipose tissue scarring

This set is mostly **mechanistic**, not diagnostic. These genes/pathways should be interpreted by expression/protein/activity in tissue, not as simple Mendelian lipedema genes.

| Gene/family | Function | Relevance to lipedema-like phenotype | Evidence category |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TGFB1 / TGFB pathway** | Profibrotic cytokine signaling; fibroblast/myofibroblast activation; collagen deposition | Explains progressive fibrosis, nodules, tissue firmness, pain, and reduced tissue plasticity | Strong pathway plausibility; not gene-specific causal proof |
| **MMP2 / MMP9 / MMP14** and **TIMPs** | Matrix degradation/remodeling, basement membrane and perivascular ECM regulation | Imbalance can create stiff/disorganized ECM, alter angiogenesis and endothelial-perivascular support; vascular review notes COL1A1/COL3A1 with reduced MMP9 activity as a microvascular fragility mechanism | Lipedema ECM remodeling evidence supports pathway; exact gene direction may vary by cell type/stage |
| **LOX / LOXL2** | Collagen/elastin crosslinking and matrix stiffening | Increased crosslinking could lock in fibrosis, raise tissue stiffness and pain, and impair fluid drainage | Mechanistic candidate; direct lipedema evidence limited |
| **FN1 / fibronectin** | Provisional ECM, cell adhesion, wound repair, angiogenesis/fibrosis | Could support stromal cell adhesion, endothelial remodeling, macrophage-fibroblast interaction | Mechanistic candidate; check omics datasets |
| **POSTN / periostin** | Matricellular profibrotic protein; fibroblast activation and tissue remodeling | Could link chronic inflammation/repair to fibrosis and pain | Mechanistic candidate; not established lipedema gene |
| **COL1A1 / COL3A1 / COL6A1-3** | Fibrillar/pericellular adipose ECM | Collagen accumulation and altered organization are central to lipedema fibrosis and adipocyte mechanical stress | Tissue-level evidence; COL3A1 also differential for vEDS |

### Mechanistic model

1. Capillary leak or lymphatic inefficiency increases interstitial protein/fluid.
2. Macrophages and fibroblasts shift tissue toward repair/remodeling.
3. TGFB and matricellular programs increase collagen/fibronectin/periostin.
4. MMP/TIMP imbalance and LOX crosslinking reduce matrix turnover and stiffen tissue.
5. Stiffer ECM worsens pain, lymphatic compression/flow, adipocyte stress, and microvascular fragility.

This model fits lipedema progression from soft swelling to nodular/fibrotic tissue, but it predicts **stage- and cell-type-specific expression**, not one static causal variant.

## 6) Practical classification: differential vs shared mechanism

### A. High-priority differential diagnoses / phenocopies

- **Primary lymphedema:** FLT4, VEGFC, FOXC2, GJC2, PIEZO1, CCBE1, ADAMTS3, EPHB4, SOX18, PROX1/CELSR1-like panels.
- **Vascular/adipose overgrowth syndromes:** PIK3CA-related overgrowth spectrum, AKT1 Proteus, PTEN hamartoma tumor syndrome/SOLAMEN, TEK venous malformations.
- **Connective-tissue disorders:** TNXB-related classical-like EDS, COL5A1/COL5A2 classical EDS, COL3A1 vascular EDS, FBN1/ELN vascular-elastic disorders.

These are most relevant when the phenotype is congenital, segmental/asymmetric, malformation-rich, pedal, systemic, or has major hypermobility/skin/vascular red flags.

### B. Plausible shared mechanisms/modifiers in classical lipedema

- **VEGF-C/VEGFR3/FLT4 and VEGF-D:** lymphangiogenic compensation/permeability balance.
- **TEK/Tie2/ANGPT and endothelial junctions:** capillary stability, leak, bruising.
- **PI3K-AKT-mTOR activity:** adipocyte/stromal growth and survival; pathway convergence rather than necessarily mosaic overgrowth.
- **TNXB/ELN/collagen architecture:** tissue compliance, vessel support, bruising, edema reservoir capacity.
- **TGFB-MMP-LOX-FN1-POSTN ECM program:** progression to fibrosis, nodules, stiffness and pain.

### C. What should not be overclaimed

- Do **not** present FLT4/FOXC2/GJC2/PIEZO1/CCBE1/ADAMTS3/EPHB4/PROX1/SOX18 as established lipedema genes. They are best framed as lymphedema differentials and lymphatic pathway modifiers.
- Do **not** present PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN/TEK as classical lipedema genes unless the phenotype shows mosaic overgrowth/vascular malformation and affected-tissue testing supports it.
- Do **not** infer COL5A1/COL5A2/COL3A1/FBN1/ELN causality from easy bruising alone. These are important red-flag differentials.
- Do **not** treat TGFB/MMP/LOX/FN1/POSTN as germline causal candidates without variant/segregation data; they are stronger as tissue-state markers.

## 7) Recommended next analyses for the parent project

1. **Create a two-tier gene table**: direct lipedema evidence vs cross-condition differential/modifier. Put the requested genes largely in tier 2 except VEGFC/VEGFD/FLT4/TEK/TNXB where lipedema-specific evidence exists.
2. **Phenotype-stratify cohorts** before genetic interpretation:
   - typical symmetric lipedema without foot edema;
   - lipedema + objective lymphedema;
   - lipedema + hypermobility/easy bruising;
   - asymmetric/segmental overgrowth or vascular malformation.
3. **Testing logic**:
   - Germline panel for primary lymphedema/EDS/PTEN if clinical clues exist.
   - Affected-tissue deep sequencing for PIK3CA/AKT1/TEK if mosaic overgrowth/VM clues exist.
   - RNA/protein activity assays for TGFB/MMP/LOX/FN1/POSTN and endothelial-junction/VEGF modules in typical lipedema.
4. **Do not mix controls**: compare lipedema separately against obesity, primary lymphedema, EDS/hypermobility, and PROS/VM where possible.
